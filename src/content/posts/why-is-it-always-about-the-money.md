It’s Friday night, and you are in the mood to order some pizza.

> **You:** Dad, I am ordering pizza. Would you like any?  
> **Your dad:** Why do you want to order pizza? We have lot of food at home.

Here we go again. Whenever you order something home to eat, your parents would complain about how expensive it is. They believe that you are wasting money. Even if you offer them some of the food, they would happily accept.

You don’t get it:

_What are they complaining about if they did not have to spend any money, and they enjoy the food just as much as I do?_

And yet, they eat, complain about it at the same time, and tell you not to waste your money like that next time.

---

## It’s the same questions:

- Do you need to buy that?
- Why do you want to buy that? Is it necessary? Do you need it?
- Do you really need to spend money on that? What do you need it for?

---

## It’s the same messages:

- Don’t spend too much money.
- You need to save for rainy days. We don’t know what the future holds.
- With the unpredictable economy like now, how can you be sure that your job is guaranteed?
- If you want to have a family of your own one day, you should start saving for a house down payment.

It’s the constant worry about money.

And you are tired of it.

---

You understand that your parents came here to Canada with nothing. They had to start from scratch with nobody to rely on. With two little ones to take care of, it made more sense for your mom to stay home and take care of you and your sister.

Your dad had to work two jobs to make ends meet. Growing up, you only saw him on the weekend, and all he did at home was watch TV. He was so drained and exhausted from his manual labor jobs that he had no energy left to check up on you kids.

For your parents, money was tight.

You overheard them arguing about money. Mom would ask for more money for groceries as your appetites kept expanding as you grew. On top of that, family members back home would call from time to time asking for money.

It could be anything:

- An uncle needing money for urgent surgery
- An auntie passing away and money needed for a funeral
- A wedding contribution

Mom would not spend anything on herself. She would wear the clothes that dad did not want anymore. For her, clothing was not important. As long as it did not have any holes, it was good enough.

She saved every penny and sent money back home whenever she could.

She always told you:

> It’s a blessing that we can come here to Canada. We have the duty to support those back home who don’t have. They depend on us.

Dad would tell her to make do. He already worked two jobs. What else could he do?

It was a constant fight about money.

---

Growing up, you never went out to eat as a family. The only place you could go for special occasions was McDonald’s. Whenever you got a Happy Meal, it made your day. You treasured the little toy so much and played with it for days.

You learned to go without things.

Mom would give you bus fare money to get to school and back. You walked instead and saved that money to buy toys or snacks. The moment you could work, you asked your mom if you could deliver newspapers. You found any odd jobs that would make you money.

You were never afraid of working.

In fact, you worked hard in school to get a university degree. And now, thanks to all your efforts, you landed a very good job with lots of benefits.

Life is different now compared to when they first came here.

You get paid more in a month than what your dad made in a year. You make more than enough for yourself and still save on the side. You know you are financially responsible.

You’re still single. No commitments yet. One day, you might want to marry and move out. But until then, you still want to spend time with your parents.

You contribute to the house, but it’s nothing compared to paying rent outside. Your mom still cooks your favorite meals. She still does the laundry for you. Your parents are happy that you still live at home.

And you want to enjoy your hard-earned money with them while you can.

You think they should be proud of you.

But instead, it’s the constant reminder to spend wisely — or better yet, not spend at all.

---

It hurts whenever your parents talk to you about saving.

For you, it is a sign that they do not trust your decision-making.

You might ask yourself:

- Why don’t they trust me for once?
- When would they trust me?
- I have always been so responsible, yet they still don’t trust me.
- This is so frustrating. This is my money. This is my life.
- I am tired of them nagging me about saving all the time.
- Am I not good enough for them?

---

## What can you do?

There are a few things that might help:

### 1. Understand their relationship with money

Your parents tie money to their sense of security. They never had financial security when they first came here. With so little, there was not much to go around. Lack of money was associated with stress and worry.

So now, even though things are better, they still feel the need to save — just in case.

---

### 2. Respond calmly and clearly

The next time they nag you, look them in the eyes, smile, and calmly say:

> “Thank you, Dad (or Mom), for trying to look out for me. I appreciate that you always want the best for me. I know that you want me to save as much as I can so I can have a good future. But what I really need right now is for you to believe in me. I want you to trust my decision making. I got this.”

If they continue, you can add:

> “I know you mean well, Dad (or Mom). And I do appreciate your concern. However, if you continue to nag me about this, I will need to stop talking. I hope you understand, but I do not want to engage in this conversation anymore. All I need from you right now is to trust me with my money. Allow me to make mistakes if I have to. I need to learn it for myself, not just by being constantly told what to do.”

---

### 3. Be consistent

It might feel awkward at first. But if you mean what you say and say it calmly and collectively, they will eventually get the message.

They might change the topic.  
They might go quiet.  
They might still repeat themselves.

If needed, remove yourself from the room and simply say:

> “I gotta go. Sorry.”

---

### 4. Teach them how to support you

They might never change how they relate to money. They might still believe that telling you what to do is how they show love.

But you can change how the conversation goes.

When you express appreciation for their intention, you show them you understand they speak out of love.

When you tell them what you need to hear instead, you teach them how to support you.

They love you deeply. Sometimes they just do not know how to relate to you as you grow older.

You still want their love.

But you don’t need them to micro-manage you.

You need their trust and moral support more than their lectures.

So help them by giving them the words you want to hear — and ask them to repeat them back to you.

Once they realize you are trying to improve the relationship, they will be more open to change.

And you will have a much healthier relationship with your old folks.
